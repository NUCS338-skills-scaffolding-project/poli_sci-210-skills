This week for the article we were tasked with reading, we were given Professor Gilbert’s
piece on kidnapping in Colombia by guerilla forces. In her work Professor Gilbert very adeptly
shows how kidnapping for ransom was utilized by these guerilla forces to extract taxation from
the local populations. She starts her paper by explaining the prior literature on the causes of
kidnapping as a tool of political violence. She lays out a table of possible reasons, perpetrators,
and outcomes from a series of explanations on why groups use kidnapping as a tool. For her
research, Professor Gilbert utilized a series of interviews with former combatants and subject
experts about the use of kidnapping in these organizations. She intentionally sought out a wide
range of individuals from different parts of these organizations, that parted with these
organizations on different terms, and compared them with responses from subject experts. From
her interviews she found that these guerilla groups were clearly using kidnapping as part of a
taxation racket wherein the kidnapping worked as both a deterrent and compellent force. To not
be kidnapped, the civilians just had to pay, and if they didn’t they or one of their loved ones
would be kidnapped and held for ransom which would then secure the money lost by the person
not paying taxes.
​
While not as quantitatively rich as many of the other articles we have read thus far, I feel
that Gilbert did an excellent job at proving a causal chain through the ample qualitative evidence
she gives. She makes sure to lay out four observable characteristics of these groups if they
were using kidnapping as a form of tax enforcement. First, these groups that rely on regularized
taxation should be seen to kidnap more than groups that do not. She shows this with the
number of kidnappings that were perpetrated by ELN and FARC two guerilla groups when
compared with other groups such as paramilitary groups. Another characteristic would be the
fact that they would target people who would not otherwise pay the taxes. This was presented in
both cases of multinational corporations that were forced to pay heavy taxes to protect
themselves from repeated kidnappings that were perpetrated by the ELN and FARC. Third, she
argues there would be some internal infrastructure to facilitate these kidnappings, which were
present in both groups. Finally she argues that there should be a change in behavior as a
response to these kidnappings. This is the hardest point to prove of the four which she does
acknowledge and point for further research. It is hardest to prove this because the territory
these groups hold is highly variable and so the area they are using kidnapping to enforce taxes
on changes wildly. I feel overall she is very adequately able to point to clear observations from
her interviews that validate and show these traits are present.
​
I believe that Professor Gilbert is also very good at examining and refuting alternative
explanations for the findings. For example when she discusses ideology as a backing for these
kidnappings instead of coercive backing for taxation, she points out that there are many other
left guerilla groups that do not do similar behavior even though they share similar ideology.
​
The only major criticism I have with this paper is that I am not sure how applicable it
would be outside of the Colombian context. As she mentions the taxation systems were in place
on the various valuable resources that Colombia has such as emeralds, coca, and poppy. This
meant that for the legally traded items there were large multinational corporations that were
extracting them from Colombia. One of these is the case of Chiquita where this US-based
company paid these groups the taxes rather than face repeated kidnappings of their workers.
Since these large corporations would not bother paying the tax any other way, and can’t move
their operations due to resource location or climate or environmental reasons, they instead pay

the tax to stop the violence. In a country where there weren’t large multinational corporations
that would pay this price in such a steady fashion, I do not know if these findings would hold.
Even this idea Professor Gilbert may refute in her paper, but I feel overall further study into the
use of kidnappings as a political tool and its various motives is very interesting and essential.
